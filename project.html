What is our Project

2014/09/01

BACKGROUND

Although various chemical transportation occurs in a liquid phase, gas exchange is also an indispensable part to be considered. Gas molecules play significant roles globally i.e. global warming and microscopically i.e. photosynthesis. Thus, gaining access to controlling gaseous chemical exchange will provide substantial benefits in many aspects. With the development of DNA nanotechnology, novel DNA nanostructures can be designed based on Watson-Crick base pairing. Different structures have already been realized such as DNA origami. This has opened up a range of opportunities to tackle various problems by exploiting the specificity and biocompatibility of DNA molecules. At this time, we initiated an idea to utilize the advantages of DNA nanostructures in creating a gas passage-controlling system. With inspiration from plant stomata and a vending machine's coin slot, we proposed a DNA origami polymer that facilitates light-inducible gas exchange through the membrane of a soap bubble. We hope to open up a new paradigm of DNA nanotechnology in gas exchange systems.


OUR QUESTION

Many attempts have been made to design DNA nanopores as a selective gating of molecules through lipid bilayers, mimicking the transmembrane nanostructures in living cells. However, in order to design the structure for gas exchange, an inversion of the structure occurs. In contrast to the lipid bilayer, though having a similar structure, the hydrophobic tails of the soap’s bilayer face outward and the hydrophilic heads form the inside. In addition, the thickness of the bubble can be varied from 10 nanometers to over 1000 nanometers. Hence, alternative approaches are required. Moreover, the size of gas molecules is another challenge. Carbon dioxide, composed of only three atoms, is much smaller than the size of typical biochemical compounds. To perfectly control the gas passage, careful design needs to be done.
